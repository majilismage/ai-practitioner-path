AI Capability Map

Understanding what AI can and can't do (and why it matters for strategy)

üìã Briefing

The biggest mistake professionals make with AI isn't technical ‚Äî it's strategic. They either expect too much (AI can't read minds or understand unspoken context) or too little (AI can actually handle complex reasoning and creative tasks).

This isn't about memorizing model specifications. It's about developing intuition for where AI adds value vs. where human expertise is irreplaceable. When you understand these boundaries, you can design workflows that amplify your strengths instead of fighting AI's limitations.

The goal is to build a mental map: Given this type of task, in this context, with this level of risk, should you use AI, avoid it, or use it in a specific way?

üéØ Core

Where AI Excels:

Pattern Recognition & Analysis:

  • Identifying trends in large datasets
  • Spotting anomalies or outliers
  • Comparing similar items (contracts, resumes, proposals)
  • Extracting structured data from unstructured text

Content Generation & Transformation:

  • First drafts of written content
  • Reformatting information for different audiences
  • Summarizing complex documents
  • Brainstorming ideas and alternatives

Systematic Processing:

  • Applying consistent criteria across many items
  • Following detailed processes or checklists
  • Quality checking against established standards
  • Routine calculations and analysis

Where AI Struggles:

Contextual Nuance:

  • Understanding unspoken organizational politics
  • Interpreting cultural or industry-specific subtleties
  • Reading between the lines in conversations
  • Knowing when to break rules or make exceptions

Real-World Integration:

  • Understanding the current state of dynamic situations
  • Accessing private or proprietary information
  • Making decisions with incomplete information
  • Coordinating with other people or systems

High-Stakes Judgment:

  • Legal or regulatory compliance decisions
  • Strategic business decisions with major consequences
  • Personnel decisions (hiring, firing, promotions)
  • Anything where being wrong creates significant liability

The Strategic Framework:

High Confidence Use Cases:

  • Task is well-defined and repeatable
  • Success criteria are clear and measurable
  • Mistakes are easily caught and corrected
  • You can provide sufficient context

Human-AI Collaboration:

  • AI generates options, you choose
  • AI does first pass, you refine
  • AI handles volume, you handle exceptions
  • AI provides analysis, you make decisions

Human-Only Territory:

  • Ambiguous situations requiring judgment
  • High-stakes decisions with major consequences
  • Tasks requiring real-world context you can't easily explain
  • Situations where being wrong is costly

Model Selection Guide:

Claude (Anthropic):

  • Best for: Complex reasoning, long documents, creative tasks
  • Strengths: Nuanced understanding, helpfulness, safety
  • Use when: Quality and accuracy matter more than speed

GPT-4 (OpenAI):

  • Best for: General tasks, creative writing, coding
  • Strengths: Versatility, speed, broad capabilities
  • Use when: You need quick, general-purpose assistance

Claude Code:

  • Best for: Development work, data analysis, technical tasks
  • Strengths: Tool integration, file system access, execution environment
  • Use when: You need AI that can actually run and test code

Your Assignment:

Step 1: Audit Your Current AI Use

For the next week, track every time you use AI (ChatGPT, Copilot, Claude, etc.):

  • What was the task?
  • How satisfied were you with the result? (1-5 scale)
  • How much did you need to edit or refine the output?
  • Would you use AI for this task again?

Step 2: Identify Missed Opportunities

Look at your typical work week and identify 3-5 tasks that you currently do manually that might be good AI candidates:

  • Are they repetitive or systematic?
  • Do they involve pattern recognition or content transformation?
  • Would mistakes be easy to catch?
  • Can you provide clear instructions and context?

Step 3: Run Capability Tests

Pick 2-3 tasks from your "missed opportunities" list and test them with AI:

Test Structure:
  1. Define the task clearly
  2. Provide context and examples
  3. Specify your quality criteria
  4. Run the test with your preferred AI model
  5. Evaluate the output against your criteria
Example Test:

Task: "Review this vendor proposal and identify potential risks"
Context: "We're considering this software vendor for a 3-year contract. Focus on technical, financial, and operational risks."
Quality Criteria: "Should identify at least 3 specific risks with severity assessment and mitigation suggestions."

Step 4: Map Your Personal AI Strategy

Based on your tests and audit, create three lists:

Green Light (High Confidence):

Tasks where AI consistently delivers good results with minimal editing

Yellow Light (Collaboration):

Tasks where AI provides a useful starting point but requires significant human input

Red Light (Human Only):

Tasks where AI either fails or where the risk of mistakes is too high

ü§î Debrief

Pattern Recognition:

Looking at your results, what patterns do you notice?

  • Which types of tasks consistently worked well with AI?
  • Where did AI struggle in ways you didn't expect?
  • What surprised you about AI's capabilities (positively or negatively)?

Strategic Insights:

  • How much time could you realistically save by systematically using AI for your "Green Light" tasks?
  • What would you do with that time if AI handled more routine work?
  • Which "Yellow Light" tasks could become "Green Light" with better prompting or different AI models?

Boundary Awareness:

  • Where did you discover important limitations you hadn't considered?
  • What tasks did you initially think were AI-suitable but turned out not to be?
  • How has this changed your mental model of where AI fits in your work?

The Meta-Learning:

The goal isn't to maximize AI usage ‚Äî it's to optimize the human-AI combination. The best AI practitioners aren't those who use AI for everything, but those who know exactly when and how to use it for maximum impact.

Next Level Questions:

  • How could you systematize your "Green Light" tasks to save even more time?
  • What would change if AI capabilities improved significantly in the next 6 months?
  • How do you stay updated on AI capability improvements without getting distracted by hype?
