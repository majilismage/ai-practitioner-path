Module 3.1

Advanced Prompting Strategies

Beyond CRAFT — sophisticated techniques for complex tasks

Briefing

CRAFT prompting gets you 80% of the way there for most tasks. But when you're working on complex analysis, creative projects, or multi-step problems, you need more sophisticated techniques.

Advanced prompting isn't about longer prompts — it's about strategic prompting. It's understanding how AI "thinks" and structuring your requests to leverage its strengths while compensating for its limitations.

These techniques transform AI from a smart assistant to a thought partner capable of handling nuanced, multi-faceted challenges that require reasoning, creativity, and strategic thinking.

Core

Chain-of-Thought Reasoning:

Basic Chain-of-Thought:

"Before answering, think through this step by step."

  • Forces AI to show its reasoning process
  • Reduces errors in logical reasoning
  • Helps you identify where reasoning breaks down

Structured Chain-of-Thought:

"Use this thinking framework: 1) Define the problem, 2) Identify key variables, 3) Consider alternative approaches, 4) Evaluate trade-offs, 5) Recommend solution."

Multi-Perspective Chain-of-Thought:

"Analyze this from three perspectives: Customer, Business, and Technical. For each perspective, consider the benefits, risks, and requirements."

Few-Shot Learning:

Pattern Examples:

Instead of just describing what you want, show 2-3 examples:

"Here are examples of the analysis style I want:

Example 1: [Your example]
Example 2: [Your example]

Now apply this same style to [new situation]."

Progressive Examples:

Start simple and build complexity:

  1. Simple example with explanation
  2. Moderate complexity example
  3. Complex example (closest to your actual need)

Perspective Prompting:

Role-Based Analysis:

"Analyze this decision from the perspective of: CEO (strategic impact), CFO (financial implications), Operations Manager (implementation challenges), and Customer (user experience)."

Temporal Perspectives:

"Consider the implications across three timeframes: Immediate (next 30 days), Short-term (3-6 months), Long-term (1-2 years)."

Stakeholder Mapping:

"For each stakeholder group, identify: What they care about, What they fear, What they need to be convinced, How this change affects them."

Constraint-Based Prompting:

Resource Constraints:

"Develop solutions that work within these constraints: $50k budget, 2-person team, 90-day timeline, no new technology purchases."

Quality Constraints:

"Your analysis must: Include at least 3 data sources, Address potential counterarguments, Provide specific success metrics, Include implementation timeline."

Iterative Refinement:

The Build-and-Critique Method:

  1. Generate: "Create an initial solution to this problem."
  2. Critique: "Now identify the 3 biggest weaknesses in this solution."
  3. Refine: "Revise the solution to address these weaknesses."
  4. Validate: "Test this solution against these specific scenarios."

Red Team/Blue Team:

  1. Blue Team: "Develop the best case for this strategy."
  2. Red Team: "Now argue against this strategy — what could go wrong?"
  3. Synthesis: "Considering both perspectives, what's the most robust approach?"

Meta-Prompting Techniques:

Prompt Improvement:

"Before answering my question, suggest how I could improve this prompt to get better results."

Assumption Surfacing:

"What assumptions are you making about [context/requirements/constraints] that I should validate or clarify?"

Alternative Framing:

"Reframe this problem in 3 different ways, then solve each framing."

Complex Task Decomposition:

Hierarchical Breakdown:

  1. Macro Level: "What are the 3-5 major phases of this project?"
  2. Micro Level: "For each phase, what are the specific tasks and deliverables?"
  3. Dependency Mapping: "Which tasks must be completed before others can start?"
  4. Risk Analysis: "What could go wrong at each phase, and how would you mitigate it?"

Parallel Processing:

For complex analysis:

  1. Divide: "Split this analysis into [quantitative data, qualitative insights, strategic implications]."
  2. Analyze: "Complete each analysis independently."
  3. Integrate: "Synthesize these analyses into unified recommendations."

Quality Assurance Prompting:

Self-Validation:

"After completing this analysis, check your work against these criteria: [logic consistency, evidence quality, completeness, practical feasibility]."

Stress Testing:

"Test your recommendations against these challenging scenarios: [best case, worst case, most likely case, black swan event]."

Exercise

Your Assignment:

Choose a Complex Challenge:

Select a real business problem you're facing that involves:

  • Multiple stakeholders with different priorities
  • Several potential solutions with trade-offs
  • Significant uncertainty or risk
  • Resource constraints

Technique 1: Multi-Perspective Chain-of-Thought

Prompt Template:

"I need to analyze [your challenge]. Use this structured approach:

1. Problem Definition: Clearly state the core problem and why it matters.

2. Stakeholder Analysis: For each key stakeholder, identify: - Their primary concerns and success criteria - Their power/influence level - What they need to support a solution

3. Solution Generation: Develop 3 distinctly different approaches, considering: - Resource requirements - Implementation difficulty - Risk profile - Timeline

4. Trade-off Analysis: For each solution, analyze: - What you gain - What you sacrifice - What could go wrong - What success looks like

5. Recommendation: Based on this analysis, recommend the best path forward with specific next steps."

Technique 2: Red Team/Blue Team Analysis

Take your preferred solution from Technique 1 and run it through adversarial analysis:

Blue Team Prompt:

"Act as a strong advocate for [your preferred solution]. Build the most compelling case possible, addressing: - Why this is the best approach - Evidence supporting this choice - How it addresses stakeholder concerns - Why alternatives are inferior"

Red Team Prompt:

"Now act as a skeptical critic of [same solution]. Identify: - The strongest arguments against this approach - Potential failure modes and risks - Hidden costs or complications - Why an alternative might be better"

Synthesis Prompt:

"Considering both the advocacy and criticism above, develop a more robust version of the solution that: - Addresses the strongest criticisms - Maintains the key benefits - Includes specific risk mitigation strategies - Has clear success metrics and checkpoints"

Technique 3: Few-Shot Learning for Implementation

Create detailed implementation guidance using examples:

"I need to communicate this decision to stakeholders. Here are examples of how I've successfully communicated difficult decisions before:

Example 1: [Describe a previous situation, the communication approach, and why it worked]

Example 2: [Another example with different stakeholder dynamics]

Based on these patterns, develop a communication strategy for this current situation, including: - Key messages for each stakeholder group - Optimal timing and channels - How to address likely objections - Follow-up plan to maintain buy-in"

Compare and Document:

After completing all three techniques:

  • How did the final solution differ from your initial instinct?
  • Which technique surfaced the most valuable insights?
  • What risks or considerations did you miss in your original thinking?
  • How confident are you in the refined solution vs. your original approach?

Debrief

  • Which advanced prompting techniques felt most valuable for your thinking process?

Practical Application:

  • Which of these techniques would be most useful in your regular work?
  • Where would the extra time investment in sophisticated prompting pay off?
  • What types of decisions warrant this level of analytical rigor?

  • How has your relationship with AI changed from basic prompting to advanced techniques?
  • Where do you still prefer pure human thinking vs. AI-assisted analysis?
  • What's the optimal balance between AI structure and human intuition?

Quality and Confidence:

  • Which stakeholders would be more likely to support thoroughly-analyzed recommendations?
  • What's the risk of over-analyzing vs. under-analyzing with these tools?

The Meta-Skill:

Advanced prompting isn't really about AI — it's about thinking more systematically. These techniques force you to be explicit about assumptions, consider multiple perspectives, and stress-test ideas. AI just makes this level of analytical rigor practical for everyday decisions.

Scaling Questions:

  • Which types of business decisions in your organization would benefit from systematic AI analysis?
  • How could you share these analytical approaches with your team?
  • What's the ROI of spending more time on upfront analysis vs. faster implementation?
  • How do you balance thoroughness with agility in a fast-moving business environment?
